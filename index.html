Call for Participation

Self-Expression, Self-Actualization, and Media Experimentation (SESAME):

A Dialogue and Ruminations

A symposium co-located with the ASIS&T 2026 Annual Meeting
Bangkok, Thailand • November 2026

Sponsors: U.S. West Chapter, SIG-USE



Social media platforms provide opportunities for people to connect not only with others, but also with themselves. Posting to and engaging with social media can be viewed as a form of active self-making — through content creation and content feed curation, people can form better understandings of themselves by actively constructing and exploring different realities and subsequently evaluating their own experiences (Takahashi, 2010). In online spaces, the interactions that contribute to identity and personality development are hyper-responsive and hyper-mediated: social feedback is nearly instantaneous and can be amplified by the platform itself, becoming a form of currency that is used, whether consciously or subconsciously, as a component of self-reflective identity formation processes.

However, there is tremendous diversity in how people may interact with and engage in self-expression using digital media. Though some might use video or images, such as creating TikToks or uploading to Instagram, others may vent their frustrations, debate topics, or engage in discussions via text-based mediums, like Reddit. Some may tell the story of their health challenges online, others may be more focused on professional or personal self-representation, and still others may see social media as "civic laboratories" for political expression.

Not all of us produce visible traces of ourselves online; there are yet others who receive and consume information through these social networks and use it to inform their own experiences, perspectives, and understandings of themselves. What stories do we all have to tell? In this symposium, we invite everyone to contribute to a collective exploration of self-making and communication through different forms of media. Relevant considerations include:

  • How do people express themselves online and contribute to their own online experiences? To what extent do these forms of self-expression change over time?
  • What are the processes that underlie identity formation and development in online spaces? To what extent are they iterative, dynamic, or even static?
  • How does the structure and design of different social media platforms mediate self-expression online? How have these platform-mediated venues and self-reflective processes changed over time?
  • What can we gather from the self-expressive content and interactions on social media platforms (e.g. genuine expressions of self vs. formatted, algorithm-minded content vs. reposts/remakes of other users' content)?
  • What might the future of self-expression online look like, given the general motivations and direction of social media platforms? How do these motivations aid or hinder users in relation to self-expression?
  • What are the important considerations for information and library science professionals as these platforms evolve? How can information- and behavioral frameworks inform our understanding?

 

ABOUT THE SYMPOSIUM & PRE-SYMPOSIUM PARTICIPATORY EXPERIENCE

This symposium is an in-person event co-located at the ASIS&T 2026 annual meeting. We seek to bring together researchers, practitioners, and students interested in exploring the intersection of media and self-expression, aiming to welcome a diverse audience of experiences, perspectives, and disciplines. No technical expertise or prior formalized experience with research in this space is required.

Additionally, we encourage interested attendees to engage in a participatory experience in the timeframe leading up to the symposium, where they will have an opportunity to create their own social media content as a way to engage with self-expressive and self-reflective processes firsthand. This symposium strives to be a venue to discuss existing work and foster a collective dialogue around this subject area.

Participation instructions for Options A and B will be available via this website. Check back soon — instructions going live shortly!

 

SUBMISSION & PARTICIPATION OPTIONS

We will accept three different types of submissions in preparation for this event. Attendees may also participate in the symposium without engaging in Options A through C below.

Option A — "Demo"
Participants engage in the pre-symposium content creation experience and submit the content they created, which will be used as part of a small-group collaborative and explorative activity on the day of the symposium.

Option B — "Reflection"
Should participants engage in the pre-symposium content creation experience but choose not to submit their self-created content, we invite participants to instead submit a reflection on their experience participating in the activity.

For Options A and B, participants will be provided with participation instructions via this website. (Instructions coming soon!)

Option C — "Classic"
Participants can submit a short paper or poster on a topic that aligns with the theme of the symposium without participating in the pre-symposium content creation activity. This can share completed research, research-in-progress, or exploratory academic analyses, synthesis, or commentary.

Option C submissions should use the ASIS&T template. Participants can submit:

  • Short papers (up to 4 pages, not including references)
  • Posters (up to 2 pages, not including references)
The submission deadline is August 3, 2026. Submission instructions will be posted soon.

Option D — "Observer"
Participants can attend the symposium without engaging in any of the options above.
